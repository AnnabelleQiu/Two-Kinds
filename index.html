If you were ever forced to learn piano, this story will hit home.

Jing-mei was expected to be a prodigy.
Her mother believed in the American dream—she just wanted to be herself.
Amy Tan’s Two Kinds isn’t just about conflict—it’s about silence, sacrifice, and love without words.

Jing-mei’s Journey

Childhood Hope

Her mother sees American TV prodigies and believes Jing-mei can become one too.

Pressure Builds

Jing-mei starts piano lessons she never asked for. Her mother believes hard work will guarantee success.

Failed Recital

After little practice, Jing-mei embarrasses herself at a talent show. Her mother remains silent, but expectations stay.

Rebellion

Jing-mei finally explodes: “I wish I were dead. Like them.” A deep crack forms between them.

Years Later

Jing-mei touches the keys again—not to perform, but to remember. The piano never left her; it simply waited.

Mother & Daughter: Words Left Behind

“Why can’t I be myself?”

Jing-mei, the narrator, is a Chinese-American girl caught between her mother’s expectations and her desire to be herself. She is strong-willed, introspective, and yearning to be heard.

“Only two kinds of daughters...”

Jing-mei’s mother, a Chinese immigrant, believes in the American dream but expresses love through discipline and sacrifice. Her silence hides a lifetime of loss.
